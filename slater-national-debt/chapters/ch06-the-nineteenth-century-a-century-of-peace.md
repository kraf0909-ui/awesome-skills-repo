# Chapter 6: The Nineteenth Century: A Century of Peace

## Core Idea
The nineteenth century demonstrates Slater's most important historical lesson: very large debt burdens can shrink mainly through peace, growth, and time rather than dramatic repayment.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Growth-led debt relief**:
  - When to use: when asking how an apparently crushing debt became manageable.
  - How: compare debt to economic growth over long periods.
- **Peace dividend in fiscal history**:
  - When to use: when understanding debt reduction after prolonged conflict.
  - How: identify how peace lowers emergency spending and lets the economy compound.

## Key Concepts
- Post-1815 adjustment
- Growth effect
- Mill and Ricardo debates

## Mental Models
- Compound growth can outperform moralistic debt panic.
- The burden of debt depends on the denominator as much as the numerator.

## Anti-patterns
- Assuming the nineteenth-century improvement came from aggressive principal repayment.
- Ignoring the macro effect of long peace.

## Worked Example
Slater shows that Britain did not erase its debt stock dramatically; rather, economic growth and stability made the burden look very different by 1914.

## Key Takeaways
1. Growth can be the main route out of heavy debt.
2. Long peace changes debt arithmetic profoundly.
3. Debt history should be read dynamically, not as a static stock.
