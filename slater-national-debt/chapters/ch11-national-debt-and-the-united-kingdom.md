# Chapter 11: National Debt and the 'United' Kingdom?

## Core Idea
The debt belongs to a political entity whose boundaries and internal structure have changed over time. Slater uses union, devolution, Ireland, and local authorities to show that debt is inseparable from constitutional geography.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Debt depends on state boundaries**:
  - When to use: when the political unit itself changes.
  - How: ask which territory and level of government the debt actually belongs to.
- **Public-sector boundary problem**:
  - When to use: when deciding whether local or devolved borrowing counts as national debt.
  - How: inspect guarantees, control, and accounting boundaries.

## Key Concepts
- Union and secession questions
- Local authorities
- Public-sector scope

## Mental Models
- Debt is a legal-political claim structure, not just an economic aggregate.
- Changing constitutional arrangements can reframe the meaning of the same liabilities.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating "Britain" as a timeless fiscal unit.
- Ignoring local and quasi-public borrowing because it sits outside one headline number.

## Worked Example
Slater's discussion of Scotland, Ireland, and local authorities shows how questions of who owns and who guarantees debt become politically explosive when constitutional structures shift.

## Key Takeaways
1. Debt analysis must respect political boundaries.
2. Local and devolved obligations complicate the headline picture.
3. Constitutional change can reopen old debt questions.
