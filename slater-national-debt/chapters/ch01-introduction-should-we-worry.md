# Chapter 1: Introduction: Should We Worry?

## Core Idea
Slater opens by asking why national debt provokes so much anxiety and why public debate on it is so often confused. The main problem is that people import personal-finance instincts into a macro-historical question where they only partly apply.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Debt is a burden only in context**:
  - When to use: when judging whether headline debt is dangerous.
  - How: compare debt to GDP, servicing cost, and fiscal capacity rather than looking at the stock alone.
- **Household analogy as partial guide**:
  - When to use: when debt is explained in everyday moral terms.
  - How: note what the analogy captures and what it misses about a sovereign state.

## Key Concepts
- Debt/GDP
- Interest burden
- Rollover

## Mental Models
- A government can often roll debt indefinitely if conditions remain credible.
- The right question is sustainability, not simple repayment date.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating public debt as identical to household debt.
- Inferring danger from a large nominal number alone.

## Worked Example
Slater contrasts seemingly alarming debt levels with cases where rollover and growth made them tolerable, showing that panic often comes from the wrong metric.

## Key Takeaways
1. National debt cannot be judged sensibly in raw pounds alone.
2. Sustainability matters more than simple repayment rhetoric.
3. Public debate often begins from misleading analogies.
