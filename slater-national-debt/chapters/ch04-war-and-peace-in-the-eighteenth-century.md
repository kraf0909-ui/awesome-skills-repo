# Chapter 4: War and Peace in the Eighteenth Century

## Core Idea
Britain's eighteenth-century debt history is dominated by repeated wars. Slater uses this period to show how wartime borrowing became normal, and how economists like Hume and Smith viewed the debt with deep suspicion.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **War-finance ratchet**:
  - When to use: when tracing long-run debt accumulation.
  - How: identify crisis jumps followed by incomplete peace-time reversal.
- **Debt pessimism of classical observers**:
  - When to use: when reading early economist warnings.
  - How: relate their fears to rising taxes, rentier claims, and political dependence.

## Key Concepts
- Hume
- Smith
- Eighteenth-century warfare

## Mental Models
- Peace interrupts debt growth; war resets it upward.
- Contemporary debt critics often respond to visible tax and class consequences, not just abstract arithmetic.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating rising debt as detached from military strategy.
- Assuming early warnings were irrational panic rather than historically grounded concern.

## Worked Example
The chapter shows how nearly continuous warfare drove the debt up while critics increasingly feared Britain would eventually be crushed by its obligations.

## Key Takeaways
1. War is the central driver of early debt escalation.
2. Classical debt anxiety was tied to both economics and politics.
3. The eighteenth century established the debt ratchet pattern.
