# Chapter 5: Pitt's Sinking Fund and the French Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars

## Core Idea
Slater examines one of the most famous debt-reduction ideas, the sinking fund, against the brutal realities of wartime borrowing, paper-money controversy, and postwar burden.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Sinking fund discipline**:
  - When to use: when evaluating formal debt retirement mechanisms.
  - How: ask whether genuine surpluses support it or whether borrowing continues elsewhere.
- **War finance under suspended convertibility**:
  - When to use: when debt is issued amid monetary disruption.
  - How: track how inflation, paper depreciation, and repayment in harder money change the burden.

## Key Concepts
- Pitt
- Sinking fund
- Ricardo

## Mental Models
- A sinking fund is weakest when politics keeps borrowing at the same time.
- Wartime debt incurred in depreciated money can become heavier after stabilization.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating debt-retirement schemes as magic independent of fiscal reality.
- Ignoring currency regime changes when judging the burden of war debt.

## Worked Example
The sinking fund looked prudent on paper, but wartime realities repeatedly undermined it. Slater uses the episode to show how elegant debt machinery can fail against political and military pressure.

## Key Takeaways
1. Debt-reduction devices cannot substitute for actual fiscal discipline.
2. Currency conditions can alter the real burden of debt after the fact.
3. Napoleonic finance pushed Britain to one of its historic debt peaks.
