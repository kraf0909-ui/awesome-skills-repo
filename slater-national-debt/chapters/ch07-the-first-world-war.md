# Chapter 7: The First World War

## Core Idea
The First World War produced another massive debt surge and a complicated aftermath of domestic and international claims. Slater uses it to show how wars create burdens that outlast the battlefield.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **War as borrowing imperative**:
  - When to use: when asking why states rarely finance wars purely through current taxation.
  - How: compare extraordinary temporary spending with intergenerational cost-sharing arguments.
- **Postwar debt web**:
  - When to use: when international debts become politically toxic.
  - How: track how domestic borrowing, foreign loans, and reparations interlock.

## Key Concepts
- Keynes and war finance
- International indebtedness
- Postwar settlement

## Mental Models
- Wars generate not just debt stocks but tangled creditor relationships.
- Borrowing can spread the cost of conflict across generations, but not without political aftermath.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating war finance as a one-period budget event.
- Ignoring the international structure of debt after war.

## Worked Example
Slater's account of WWI shows how the debt explosion and the web of cross-border obligations poisoned the peace and shaped later instability.

## Key Takeaways
1. The First World War transformed the debt question for the twentieth century.
2. International debt arrangements can destabilize peace as well as war.
3. War borrowing creates lasting macro and political consequences.
