# Chapter 14: Conclusions

## Core Idea
Slater's conclusion is measured rather than alarmist. We should worry about debt, but in the right way: through sustainability, growth, servicing cost, and hidden obligations, not through raw moral panic over the existence of debt itself.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Conditional concern**:
  - When to use: when asked whether debt is simply good or bad.
  - How: translate the question into burden, use, and sustainability conditions.
- **Historical humility**:
  - When to use: when contemporary debate treats current debt as unprecedented.
  - How: compare today's worries with earlier peaks and their eventual resolution.

## Key Concepts
- Historical perspective
- Conditional borrowing
- Debt concern properly framed

## Mental Models
- Debt concern should be disciplined, not theatrical.
- Large debts are survivable; bad growth and bad policy are the deeper threat.

## Anti-patterns
- Alarmism detached from history.
- Complacency based only on low current market stress.

## Worked Example
Slater synthesizes the whole history to show that Britain's debt has repeatedly looked overwhelming, yet the decisive variables have been war, growth, rates, and institutional quality rather than raw debt size.

## Key Takeaways
1. Worry about debt intelligently, not ritualistically.
2. History gives perspective but not automatic reassurance.
3. Sustainability depends on policy and growth conditions.
