# Chapter 13: Is There More Than the National Debt?

## Core Idea
The conventional National Debt number is not the whole story. Slater explores hidden liabilities, assets, pensions, guarantees, and other balance-sheet items that change how we should interpret the public position.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Broader public balance sheet**:
  - When to use: when official debt figures appear reassuring or alarming in isolation.
  - How: add assets, contingent liabilities, pensions, and off-balance-sheet commitments.
- **Boundary critique**:
  - When to use: when government accounting defines away obligations.
  - How: ask what has been excluded and why.

## Key Concepts
- Contingent liabilities
- Pensions
- Whole of Government Accounts

## Mental Models
- One debt figure can both overstate and understate the real burden depending on what is being asked.
- Public accounts need both asset and liability sides.

## Anti-patterns
- Taking the conventional debt as the whole public position.
- Treating all excluded liabilities as either irrelevant or fully certain without nuance.

## Worked Example
Slater shows that pensions, public corporations, local entities, QE holdings, and guarantees can materially alter the meaning of the official debt stock.

## Key Takeaways
1. Headline debt is only one slice of the state's obligations.
2. Accounting boundaries are politically and analytically important.
3. Better debt debate requires a broader balance-sheet perspective.
