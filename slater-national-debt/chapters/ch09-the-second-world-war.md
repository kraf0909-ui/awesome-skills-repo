# Chapter 9: The Second World War

## Core Idea
The Second World War pushed debt upward again, but the postwar management of that burden differed importantly from the aftermath of the First World War.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Wartime debt with postwar management**:
  - When to use: when comparing WWI and WWII debt consequences.
  - How: assess borrowing, inflation, repression, and subsequent institutional management together.
- **Financial repression as debt tool**:
  - When to use: when low nominal rates and captive savings help contain debt burdens.
  - How: identify how states channel savings into government debt at favorable terms.

## Key Concepts
- WWII borrowing
- Inflation after war
- Financial repression

## Mental Models
- The same debt stock can play out differently under different postwar regimes.
- Managing the holder base matters as much as issuing the debt.

## Anti-patterns
- Assuming all war debts produce the same postwar trajectory.
- Ignoring the role of regulated finance in debt control.

## Worked Example
Slater contrasts the more destructive inter-war legacy of WWI with the more manageable post-1945 environment in which growth and policy control were stronger.

## Key Takeaways
1. WWII created another debt peak but a different policy environment.
2. Financial repression can quietly shift the burden onto savers and institutions.
3. Postwar debt relief can occur without formal repudiation.
