# Chapter 2: Pre-history

## Core Idea
Before there was a formal National Debt, there had to be the fiscal and political preconditions for one. Slater shows how monarchy, Parliament, taxation, and credibility had to evolve before large-scale public borrowing became durable.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Fiscal capacity before debt capacity**:
  - When to use: when asking how states become able to borrow.
  - How: trace taxation legitimacy, administrative reach, and political stability first.
- **Credibility as borrowing infrastructure**:
  - When to use: when comparing weak and strong pre-modern states.
  - How: ask whether lenders believe the state can and will honour claims.

## Key Concepts
- Taxation basis
- Parliamentary consent
- State credibility

## Mental Models
- Debt markets do not appear in a vacuum; they require institutions.
- Coercion alone is weaker than credible revenue mechanisms.

## Anti-patterns
- Assuming states can borrow heavily just because they desire funds.
- Ignoring political consent in long-run debt formation.

## Worked Example
The chapter explains why earlier rulers often resorted to forced loans or ad hoc finance: the durable institutional base for funded debt had not yet been built.

## Key Takeaways
1. National debt presupposes institutional maturity.
2. Tax legitimacy matters as much as borrowing need.
3. Political structure shapes financial possibility.
