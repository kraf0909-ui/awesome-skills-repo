# Chapter 15: Recent Developments (November 2024)

## Core Idea
The update chapter re-tests the book's framework against recent shocks and policy episodes. Slater brings the debt story through newer fiscal stress, market sensitivity, and long-run growth worries.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Recent shock as framework test**:
  - When to use: when deciding whether older historical lessons still apply.
  - How: evaluate debt through rates, growth, market confidence, and spending commitments.
- **Growth pessimism as debt threat**:
  - When to use: when debt looks manageable in stock terms but long-run outlook weakens.
  - How: focus on productivity and growth rather than debt stock alone.

## Key Concepts
- OBR fiscal sustainability
- Growth slowdown
- Market credibility risk

## Mental Models
- Modern debt crises can be triggered by confidence and rates even without historic debt peaks.
- Weak growth can turn a manageable debt path into a difficult one.

## Anti-patterns
- Assuming recent calm proves long-run safety.
- Ignoring the interaction between poor services, poor growth, and debt sustainability.

## Worked Example
The chapter uses recent British fiscal episodes to show that even when debt ratios are not historically unprecedented, weak growth and credibility shocks can still create serious pressure.

## Key Takeaways
1. Recent events do not overturn the historical framework; they sharpen it.
2. Growth remains the central medium-term variable.
3. Debt management depends heavily on maintaining confidence without sacrificing capacity.
