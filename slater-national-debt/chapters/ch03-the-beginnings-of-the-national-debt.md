# Chapter 3: The Beginnings of the National Debt

## Core Idea
This chapter covers the financial revolution that made Britain's National Debt a durable institution. The founding of the Bank of England and the funded-debt system transformed state borrowing.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Funded debt as institutional breakthrough**:
  - When to use: when explaining why early modern borrowing became sustainable.
  - How: show how long-term structured claims replaced improvised financing.
- **Financial revolution**:
  - When to use: when analyzing Britain's fiscal rise.
  - How: link public credit, Parliament, and financial intermediation.

## Key Concepts
- Bank of England
- Funded debt
- Public credit

## Mental Models
- Once lenders trust the state's revenue base, debt becomes scalable.
- Institutional innovation can be as important as military success.

## Anti-patterns
- Treating early debt creation as mere bookkeeping.
- Ignoring how political and financial elites became intertwined.

## Worked Example
Slater shows how a credible funded-debt system allowed Britain to finance state activity on a scale that earlier arrangements could not support reliably.

## Key Takeaways
1. The National Debt was born from institutional innovation, not only necessity.
2. Public credit changed the state's strategic capacity.
3. Early borrowing systems created long-run political controversies as well as capability.
