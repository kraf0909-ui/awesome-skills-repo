## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

### National debt is not the same as household debt
- Slater's starting point is that people instinctively reason about public debt using analogies from personal borrowing.
- He argues that this is often misleading because a state is not mortal in the way a household is, can roll debt over for centuries, and has taxation power.
- The better question is rarely "when will it be repaid?" but "under what conditions is it sustainable and at what cost?"

### Debt should usually be judged relative to the economy, not in cash size alone
- Raw debt totals look frightening but tell little by themselves.
- Slater repeatedly returns to **Debt/GDP** and **interest/GDP** as more meaningful long-run measures.
- Growth matters enormously: if the economy grows faster than the debt burden, yesterday's alarming stock can become manageable.

### Wars are the great ratchet of national debt
- Britain's debt history is driven above all by war.
- Peace may reduce burdens slowly, but major jumps come from military emergency.
- A key historical pattern is that war debts are accepted in crisis, then only partially unwound afterward.

### Growth is often the main escape route
- Large debt reductions in history often did not come primarily from heroic repayment.
- They came from a mixture of peace, economic growth, moderate inflation, and time.
- This shifts attention from moral panic about headline debt stocks toward the conditions that support productive growth.

### The debt burden depends on interest rates as much as debt levels
- A very large debt at very low rates can be easier to carry than a smaller debt at high rates.
- This is why **servicing cost** matters alongside principal.
- It also explains why changes in credibility, inflation, and policy regime alter the meaning of a given debt stock.

### Public debt has distributional and political effects
- Debt is not only a macroeconomic variable. It changes who holds claims on whom.
- Domestic debt can be one group of citizens holding claims on another via the state.
- Foreign-held debt changes the picture by turning part of the burden into an external claim.

### Official debt measures can understate or distort the public position
- Slater repeatedly warns that the conventionally reported National Debt is not the full story.
- Off-balance-sheet commitments, pensions, guarantees, local authorities, public corporations, and contingent liabilities can all matter.
- A narrow debt number may therefore either overstate danger in one sense or understate obligations in another.

### There is no single magic threshold
- Claims such as "100% of GDP is the danger line" are too simple.
- Sustainability depends on growth, interest rates, primary balances, inflation, institutional credibility, maturity structure, and political capacity.
- This makes debt analysis conditional rather than slogan-driven.

### Borrowing can be justified differently across uses
- Slater distinguishes more sympathetically between borrowing for war, for current spending, and for long-term productive investment.
- This does not mean all investment borrowing is wise; it means some borrowing creates assets or future capacity that change the judgment.
- The right test is intertemporal: who benefits, who pays, and over what horizon?

### National debt should be placed inside a broader balance-sheet view
- Governments have liabilities, but they also have assets, tax capacity, regulatory power, and future obligations.
- Looking only at one headline debt figure hides this wider public-sector balance sheet.
- Slater's later chapters push the reader to think in terms of net position, hidden obligations, and institutional context rather than one blunt number.
