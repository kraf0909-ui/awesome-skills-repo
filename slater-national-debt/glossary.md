# Glossary

**Debt/GDP ratio** - The stock of national debt measured against the size of the economy, one of Slater's preferred long-run burden measures. (Ch 1, Ch 12)

**Financial repression** - Policies that help governments finance or erode debt burdens by directing savings into government securities and holding rates down. (Ch 9, Ch 10)

**Funded debt** - Long-term debt consolidated into structured obligations, central to Britain's early debt development. (Ch 3)

**Gilt** - A British government bond. In modern usage this is the main market instrument of government borrowing. (App 1)

**Index-linked bond** - A bond whose payments are adjusted for inflation, protecting real value and changing the state's inflation incentives. (Ch 10, App 1)

**Interest burden** - The cost of servicing the debt, often more informative than the headline stock by itself. (Ch 1, Ch 12)

**National Debt Commissioners** - Historic officials and institutions involved in debt administration and government savings arrangements. (Ch 3, Ch 10)

**Primary surplus** - Budget balance before interest payments; central to debt sustainability arithmetic. (Ch 12)

**Quantitative Easing (QE)** - Central-bank purchases of government bonds that complicate interpretation of who effectively holds public debt. (Ch 13, App 1)

**Sinking fund** - A mechanism intended to retire government debt over time, famously associated with Pitt and often vulnerable to political reality. (Ch 5)

**Sustainability** - Whether debt can be carried without explosive deterioration, depending on growth, interest rates, and fiscal balances rather than one fixed threshold. (Ch 12, Ch 15)

**War-finance ratchet** - The historical tendency for wars to push debt sharply upward, with only partial reversal afterward. (Ch 4, Ch 14)
