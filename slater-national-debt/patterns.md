# Patterns

## War Raises the Debt, Peace Rarely Fully Reverses It
**When to use**: When tracking long-run debt history across centuries.
**How**: Look for sharp debt jumps during crisis, then slower and incomplete adjustment afterward.
**Trade-offs**: Strong historical pattern; may obscure rare periods of deliberate debt retirement.

## Growth Does More Work Than Heroic Repayment
**When to use**: When a huge debt stock looks impossible to reduce.
**How**: Check whether economic growth, inflation, and time lowered the burden more than actual repayment did.
**Trade-offs**: Historically realistic; can encourage complacency if growth assumptions are too optimistic.

## Headline Debt Numbers Mislead Without Context
**When to use**: When debt totals are used rhetorically.
**How**: Compare stock, servicing cost, GDP, maturity structure, inflation regime, and who holds the debt.
**Trade-offs**: Better judgment; less slogan-friendly.

## Official Debt Measures Hide Other Public Obligations
**When to use**: When a government claims the debt is manageable based on one narrow metric.
**How**: Check pensions, guarantees, local bodies, contingent liabilities, PFI-style commitments, and state-held debt.
**Trade-offs**: More complete picture; can mix hard liabilities with more contingent ones if done carelessly.

## Borrowing for Productive Assets Differs From Borrowing for Current Consumption
**When to use**: When judging whether new borrowing is justified.
**How**: Ask whether the borrowing creates durable productive capacity or merely shifts current spending forward.
**Trade-offs**: Useful distinction; still vulnerable to optimistic project labeling.

## Interest Rates Change the Meaning of the Same Debt Stock
**When to use**: When comparing debt burdens across periods.
**How**: Compare servicing costs, not just principal. A large low-rate debt may be easier than a smaller high-rate one.
**Trade-offs**: Essential for realism; can underplay refinancing risk if rates rise.

## Household Analogies Break Down Quickly
**When to use**: When public debt is discussed as if a country were a family budget.
**How**: Replace the life-cycle and repayment framing with rollover capacity, taxation power, growth, and collective claims.
**Trade-offs**: Improves macro logic; does not excuse truly unsustainable fiscal paths.
