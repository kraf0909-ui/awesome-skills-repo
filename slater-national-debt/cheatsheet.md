# Cheatsheet

## Fast Rules

| If you hear this claim | Check this first |
|---|---|
| "The debt is huge in cash terms" | Debt/GDP and interest/GDP |
| "The country must pay it all back like a household" | rollover logic, state longevity, tax capacity |
| "100% of GDP is always dangerous" | growth, rates, primary balance, credibility |
| "The headline debt tells the whole story" | pensions, guarantees, local/public entities, assets |
| "Borrowing is always bad" | what the money is financing and over what horizon |
| "Inflation solved the debt" | who bore the cost and whether repression helped |

## Slater's Default Questions

1. How large is the debt relative to GDP?
2. How large is the interest burden relative to GDP and tax revenue?
3. Who holds the debt: domestic residents, institutions, foreigners, or the state itself?
4. Is the economy growing fast enough to dilute the burden?
5. Are there hidden liabilities or off-balance-sheet obligations?
6. Is the borrowing financing war, current spending, or productive investment?

## Sustainability Test

| Variable | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Growth rate | Growth is the main long-run escape route |
| Interest rate | Determines carrying cost |
| Primary balance | Shows whether policy offsets or compounds debt drift |
| Maturity structure | Affects rollover risk |
| Inflation regime | Changes real debt burden and credibility |

## Tells and Smells

| If you see this | Suspect this |
|---|---|
| Obsession with raw debt stock alone | Missing burden context |
| Sharp wartime borrowing defended as temporary | Likely ratchet effect later |
| Claims that debt is low because one measure excludes obligations | Narrow accounting boundary |
| Low-rate environment treated as permanent | Underpriced refinancing risk |
